Backends in the wild

Backends in the wild

Robust backends recap

We're getting close now to completing our picture of how a robust backend looks. Let's quickly recap where we're at:

  • An operation timing out or getting a transient error means that the client doesn't know whether it has completed successfully.
  • By retrying until we get success confirmation, we ensure an operation eventually succeeds. But this is not free:
    • Operations need to be made idempotent, so that successful operations observed as failed can be retried without unwanted effects.
    • Since clients are usually not reliable, to really ensure success, we need to, which complicates the system.
  • It's crucial to establish a exhaustive and well-defined protocol in the form of an API that works as a contract between clients and servers.
    • All possible errors that the operations could produce must be fully defined by this protocol.
      • They must not expose irrelevant details to the client.
      • They must expose enough information for clients to handle them gracefully at the right level.
      • At the very least, application-level errors must be easily differentiated from transient errors due to system conditions.
    • Details from underlying protocols, such as TCP connection termination, should be gracefully handled and have well-defined effects.
    • When extended, the changes to the API should avoid breaking clients of the previous version.
  • The let it crash systems design principle states that, since the system must be ready to handle timeouts and crashes anyway, we can simplify error handling in a system prone to partial failure of an operation by immediately crashing in all levels of the operation when an inner-level operation fails, and letting an outermost level that reliable monitorizes the operation handle all crashes gracefully.
  • Since distributed systems are inherently concurrent, we must be on the lookout for race conditions by ensuring a well-defined order of operations.
    • Atomic operations are operations that either succeed fully or not at all, as a single event, so no concurrent events can affect their outcome.
      • Transactions is how we usually achieve atomicity in a database, but we must be very careful to choose the right isolation level to avoid assuming a more relaxed meaning of atomic.
    • Locks are the most flexible way of avoiding race conditions, but they are not atomic, so they're prone to partial failure and deadlocks (two processes waiting on each other to release a lock).
  • Since machines or disks can die at any time and data held on them can be lost, we must replicate the data in several machines/disks in order to make a durable system (a system that doesn't lose data).
    • It takes time for data to replicate, so in the meantime data can be inconsistent (two machines may contradict each other on what's the state of the system). You must choose the replication mechanism with the right consistency guarantees for your use case, taking its cost into account.
  • We can move part of the work into message queues to be done later.
    • Message queues can be made durable and usually retry failed deliveries, so we can use them to ensure an operation eventually succeeds.
    • They allow us to trade off queue length for other resources when requests rate grows.
    • They allow us to acknowledge requests as successful more quickly, but we must be careful to define that such success doesn't mean all the work has been already carried out.
  • By splitting a component of the system in several components, we can assign each of them the right resources to perform their work optimally.
    • In particular, we can split data in several databases, focused on the operations they must support, and keep them consistent through an event-based architecture.

Backends meet the physical world

The main piece missing is that backends run in real machines with limited resources and under changing conditions.

We've talked about failure in a very broad sense: what to do when anything fails. But we've seen little about how these failures, and the mechanisms we put in place to resist them, interact with the environment our backends run on.

We'll see how a good relationship with a changing environment with limited resources is the crucial missing piece for not just working around problems, but also for being able to prevent them and, even more importantly, for stopping them from spreading so much that even our recovery mechanisms break.
